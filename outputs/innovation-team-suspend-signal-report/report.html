Project Innovation Team · Internal Decision Memo · 21 May 2026

Can the prediction markets warn us to suspend a game — and how many seconds early?

When the NBA's live stats credit a rebound, assist or block to the wrong player, the bet on that player can go bad in an instant. This memo asks a simple trading question: does the betting activity on Polymarket and Kalshi flash a warning early enough, and reliably enough, that a bet365 trader could pull the affected in-play markets before we pay out on a stat the league later overturns? We tested this on NBA basketball only — but bet365 trades roughly 92 sports, the same free prediction-market feeds span most of them, and the build is shared across all of them. The basketball result is the proof of concept; the prize is the whole book.

SECTION 01

The question, in plain terms

An NBA player-prop goes bad when the league's live stat feed gives the credit to the wrong player — a rebound logged to Reaves that was really Hayes's, an assist dropped, a board credited to "the team." When that happens, can the prediction markets tell a trader fast enough to review or suspend the in-play markets on that game before the desk takes a bad position?

Why we watch the whole board, not the one bet

One stat mistake doesn't move one market — it ripples across many. A rebound credited to the wrong player nudges that player's rebound line, his points-rebounds-assists combos, the team-rebounds market, and the right player's lines. The single disputed bet is usually the quietest, thinnest market on the board. So the loudest, earliest warning is the whole board lurching at once — not the one bet drifting. That is the central idea of this whole project.

The nightmare we're guarding against

People watching the live game — on TV, or in the arena ahead of the broadcast — react on the prediction markets before the official data catches up. The dangerous case: the bettor is wrong, the NBA's feed is also wrong, both wrong → we pay out against a result the league quietly corrects days later. A live warning would let a trader suspend before we take that hit.

We are not trying to name which prop or who deserves the stat — that is impossible in seconds and isn't the goal. The output we want is a simple, fast call: "something is off with this game — look at it, or pull the flash bets." We answer in seconds of warning, in how often it cries wolf, and in a go / no-go.

SECTION 02

Bottom line, first

There is a real, measurable warning, and it arrives within seconds of the disputed play. The best single tool we found is a whole-board alarm that weights each market by how much money is on it; it confirmed our known cases 23 to 226 seconds after the disputed play, at a cry-wolf rate the desk can dial up or down. But the one number that decides the business case — how fast bet365's own desk already reacts — is not in any data we hold. So: conditional GO to a live trial that runs quietly alongside the desk; NO to flipping on automatic suspension today.

What we now know — earned from the data, not assumed:
  • Watching the whole board beats watching the one bet. Staring at the disputed bet alone misses the hardest case entirely; the narrow "watch the two players" idea caught none of the six.
  • Weighting markets by money beats treating them equally — it's the only version that caught the rebound credited to "the team."
  • Different cases show up in different places. Some only move the board; one only showed up as a single large bet. You need both alarms, not one.
  • The bet-by-bet tape isn't redundant: about a quarter of the big tell-tale bets never showed up on the slower price chart at all.
What we do not know yet — said plainly:
  • Does it beat our desk? We can't say — we have none of bet365's own reaction or suspension times. This is the single most important missing piece.
  • How reliable, and what it costs in money. Six known cases prove the warning exists and tend to arrive in the right order — not a calibrated hit-rate. The dollar cost of chasing false alarms is unmeasured.
  • The detailed bet-by-bet tape covers only 18 games (the ones we recorded live). Older games can't be pulled back.
  • Every number here is basketball. The same mechanism and the same free feeds plausibly apply to most of bet365's ~92 sports, but we have measured exactly one. The other 91 are an opportunity, not a result.

Confidence: moderate that the warning is real and arrives in the right order; low that we've measured its reliability; zero on whether it beats our own desk until we get bet365's internal timing.

SECTION 03

How we got here — the idea grew in five steps

The project didn't start at "watch the whole board." It got there because each narrower idea failed against a real case. The lesson of the whole arc: the further we widened the lens, the more we caught.

Three things we checked so the timing is honest
  • We time everything against the NBA's live play clock, not our own database timestamps — those turned out to be batch-loaded and useless for "what time did this happen."
  • We match bets to the right game by when they happened, not by the stored game label. On back-to-back nights the labels get crossed (one famous case had the bet filed under the previous night's game).
  • We threw out the noise — opening placeholder prices, impossible volume figures, and stale quotes — before measuring anything.
Why the bet-by-bet detail is only 18 games

We found and matched the other 46 games on Polymarket, but the detailed bet-by-bet history can't be downloaded after a game ends — the public feeds only give it to you live, while the game is running. The 18 games we have were all captured live. The only free way to get more is to keep recording future games live as they happen.

We also re-checked our outside sources are real and current: Polymarket's live bet feed, Kalshi's live NBA markets, the academic papers we cite, and the X.com stat-correction posts (from @nba_elise and others) that flag these misallocations all came back live. The full list with web addresses is in the appendix.

SECTION 05

How we tested it — fairly

We judged every tool the same way: how many seconds of warning it gives, at a cry-wolf rate the desk is willing to live with. The desk decides how many false alarms it can tolerate; we report how much warning you get at that level.

The moment we measure from

The disputed play itself, timed to the second from the NBA's play clock. "Warning" is how long after that play the alarm fired. Positive = after the play — i.e. how early, post-event, the desk would have known.

Our six known cases — for checking only

Six real, publicly-flagged misallocations (five rebounds, one block) we can pin to the exact second. They are only used to check the tools, never to build them — six cases can prove the warning exists and tends to arrive in the right order, but cannot prove a reliable hit-rate.

The two yardsticks

(1) How many seconds of warning on each known case. (2) The cry-wolf rate — how often the alarm fires across all 64 games when nothing is wrong. The second is the cost of automatic suspension and is the whole reason a threshold matters.

No cheating, no peeking into the future. Every alarm only uses what was knowable at that moment — it compares each minute to the game's own recent calm, never to anything that came later. We never blend Polymarket's second-by-second timing with Kalshi's coarser minute-by-minute data, and we never invent a bet365 reaction time we don't have.

6.1The scoreboard

Representative settings shown (the full sensitivity sweep lives in the data file). "Cry-wolf / min" is across all 64 games. The two narrow tools (watch the one bet, watch the two players) have no cry-wolf number — they need you to already know which market to watch, a built-in disadvantage, not an oversight.

What holds no matter how you set the dial:
  • Money-weighting always matches or beats equal-weighting, and it's the only version that caught the rebound credited to "the team." It quiets down meaningless churn in tiny markets and listens to where real money sits.
  • Watching the one bet misses the hardest case (Reaves) entirely, and watching the two players caught none of the six — the ripple is a whole-board thing, and the movers are often other players.
  • The bet-by-bet tape is worth keeping: the slower price chart only reflected a big off-price bet a typical 3¾ minutes later, and about a quarter of the time never reflected it at all.

SECTION 07

The other study — an independent paper, the same week

While we were building this, a university team published a peer-style study of the exact same markets. Our first work on this landed 21 April 2026; their paper hit arXiv 22 April 2026 — one day apart, with no contact between us. Two groups racing to the same corner of Polymarket within a day is itself a finding: this area is real, it's valuable, and we are not the only ones who see it.

The paper: "Arbitrage Analysis in Polymarket NBA Markets" — Cheng, Yang & Zou, UCLA (arXiv:2605.00864, submitted 22 Apr 2026). It asks a different question than we do: can you make guaranteed, risk-free money from price inconsistencies? — not can you spot a stat error early? It is built on a far bigger, cleaner dataset than ours: 75 million order-book snapshots across 173 games, with the full order book (every resting buy and sell offer) — depth we do not currently capture.

7.1What they found

The markets are highly efficient for risk-free profit. Guaranteed-profit gaps inside a single market are rare and vanish in a median 3.6 seconds; multi-market gaps are more common but cluster in the final minutes and return only about 1%; and the books are so shallow (~15 shares deep) that any risk-free profit is confined to tiny, retail-sized bets. In short: there is no free money here at scale.

The honest verdict: it is not "ours is better" or "theirs is better" — they answer different questions. On their question (is the market efficient enough to arbitrage?) their study is larger and more rigorous than anything we attempt, and we don't try to. On our question (an early suspend signal for mis-credited stats) the paper is silent — it has no notion of a real-world stat error, no link to the game clock, and no measure of warning-time or false alarms. Ours is purpose-built for exactly that. The two are complementary, not competing.

7.3Why this paper matters for us

1 · It backs up our design

Their finding that price gaps vanish in a median 3.6 seconds independently confirms the fast signal lives in the raw bet-by-bet tape, not the slower price chart — exactly our result that the chart lags the tape by a typical 3¾ minutes. And their finding that books are only ~15 shares deep confirms that a small, concentrated off-price bet really does move the market — which is precisely why our $100 bet at 99¢ on Reaves is a credible "someone knows something" tell, not noise.

2 · It rules out the wrong play

The paper proves there is essentially no risk-free arbitrage money here at scale. So bet365 should not chase this as a profit play — the value is exactly what we study: avoiding losses by suspending early. The paper actually strengthens our case by closing off the obvious alternative.

3 · The clock is ticking

A UCLA group — and the earlier work they cite — is already mining these markets. The data is free and others are racing, so any edge here is perishable. The flip side: they've handed us a rigorous, full-order-book baseline (the very depth we lack) to build our live capture on. Move while the edge exists.

One caveat to test in the trial: their risk-free gaps cluster in the final minutes of games — end-game noise we should make sure doesn't inflate our false-alarm count. Full notes: research/09-paper-comparison.md.

SECTION 08

Recommendation — go / no-go

Conditional GO to a quiet live trial. NO to automatic suspension today. Run the money-weighted whole-board alarm live alongside the desk, log when it fires and when the desk acts, and finally measure the one thing that decides everything: does the warning beat our own desk's clock? Only with that answer — and a real dollar cost for false alarms — is automatic suspension justified.

First, the good news: the data is essentially free

We checked the actual pricing live. Polymarket's market data is fully public — no account, no key, no fee (you only pay if you place trades, which we don't). Kalshi's market-data API is free too — you generate a key from account settings; they meter throughput with rate-limit tiers, not a per-seat data bill. We do not need Odds-API. So there is no expensive data licence to fear. Crucially, those same free feeds already span most of the ~92 sports bet365 trades — politics and econ aside, the major sports are listed — so this is not a basketball-only spend.

So where does the money actually go?
It pays off when  (money saved per caught incident) × (incidents per season, across all sports) × (how often we beat our own desk)
is bigger than  (one-off engineering + bet365 integration, spread over the year)  +  (whatever you decide a false alarm costs a trader who's already at the desk).

The real cost is engineering, not data: building the alarm, running always-on capture (the bet-by-bet tape can't be downloaded after the fact, so recorders must run live), and — the big one — wiring it into bet365's internal system, which the perspective in §9 rightly calls "a massive system that cannot mess up." That integration cost is a one-time build, shared across every sport it serves, so per-sport it shrinks the more of the 92 you turn on. We can measure the false-alarm rate (the dial below); we cannot yet measure how much an incident costs us, how many happen, or how often we'd beat our own desk — that's what the trial is for.

SECTION 09

What would make the answer certain

Three things turn this from "moderate confidence, the warning exists" into a calibrated, dollar-justified decision.

1 · Our own desk's timing

When did bet365 move its line or suspend the market, to the second? This is the single most important missing number. Without it we cannot say whether the warning beats our desk — and that decides the whole business case. David can pull bet365's internal history; this is the highest-value next step.

2 · Many more known cases

Six cases prove the warning exists; they can't prove a reliable hit-rate. A few hundred labelled incidents — from the X.com stat-correction accounts, a colleague's customer-behaviour signals, and our own review logs — would turn this into a real reliability number with error bars.

3 · Live recording of both venues

Capturing Kalshi's bet-by-bet tape and the rest of the Polymarket games live (they can't be pulled back later). Having two venues confirm each other is the single best way to cut false alarms.

4 · A second (and third) sport

Everything here is basketball. The mechanism — a live stat mis-credited, humans repricing before the official correction — should recur in any sport with live stats and a listed market. The data feeds are already free and broad, so the cheapest high-value test is to repeat this on one more sport (e.g. a football or tennis market with frequent live-stat disputes) and see if the warning holds. That is how a basketball proof-of-concept becomes a case for the whole 92-sport book.

I personally think, based on what I've seen when things are dialed right, based on @nba_elise and V and nba_stats correction calls on X.com as well as various comment threads on the prediction market sites, that there is almost certainly something here. However, we need it hooked into the bet365 internal system to get exact timing of our changes. This is obviously a great deal of time and expense potentially, because it is a massive system that cannot mess up, and going on gut instinct for such a major undertaking is not okay. engineering How the alarms are computed (formulas & scripts)

Whole-board alarm, per game: aggregate price quotes (quote_ticks) joined via source_markets.game_id; in-play window \([t_0,t_1]=\bigl[\min,\max\bigr]\) of NBA play-by-play time_actual; drop heartbeats, the \(0.500\) opening anchor, and quote gaps \(>300\,\mathrm{s}\); bin into 60-second buckets. The equal- and money-weighted bucket intensities are

\[ I^{\mathrm{eq}}_{b}=\sum_{i\in b}\bigl|\Delta p_i\bigr| \qquad\qquad I^{\mathrm{vw}}_{b}=\sum_{i\in b}\bigl|\Delta p_i\bigr|\,\ln\!\bigl(1+v_i\bigr) \]

where \(\Delta p_i\) is the change in implied probability of market \(i\) within the bucket and \(v_i\) its quote volume. A bucket fires when it exceeds a causal trailing baseline over the prior \(W=20\) buckets (after an \(8\)-bucket warm-up), using the median and median-absolute-deviation:

\[ \mathrm{fire}(b)\iff I_b \;\ge\; \operatorname{median}\bigl(I_{b-W:\,b-1}\bigr)\;+\;K\cdot\operatorname{MAD}\bigl(I_{b-W:\,b-1}\bigr) \]

with confirmation time taken at the bucket end. A single big off-price bet fires when both a volume-share and an off-price-distance gate are met:

\[ \mathrm{vol\_share}\;\ge\;0.10 \quad\wedge\quad \bigl|\,p_{\text{trade}}-p_{\text{prev}}\,\bigr|\;\ge\;0.35 \]

and the head-start of a tool on incident \(j\) is \(\ell_j = t^{\text{fire}}_j - t^{\,0}_j\) (positive = after the disputed play). The "worth-it" decision reduces to the inequality

\[ \underbrace{L\cdot N\cdot \Pr\!\bigl(\ell \ge \Delta t\bigr)}_{\text{benefit (unmeasured)}} \;\;\ge\;\; \underbrace{C_{\text{int}}\;+\;f\cdot c\cdot V}_{\text{cost (mostly measured)}} \]

\(L=\) expected loss avoided per incident, \(N=\) incidents/season, \(\Pr(\ell\ge\Delta t)=\) how often we beat our own desk by the required margin \(\Delta t\); \(C_{\text{int}}=\) amortized engineering + integration cost, \(f=\) false-alarm rate (fires/min), \(c=\) review cost, \(V=\) in-play signal volume. We measure \(f\) and \(V\); \(L,N,\Pr(\ell\ge\Delta t)\) are the trial's job.

Read-only on the 52 GB signal-console.sqlite store. Scripts: scripts/bakeoff.py, scripts/board_signal_v2.py, scripts/lead_vs_pricehist.py. Outputs: research/bakeoff-results.json, research/board-signal-v2.json.

No-future-leakage: causal trailing baseline only; last-quote-at-or-before anchoring; whole-board catch window \([-60,+300]\,\mathrm{s}\) (a bucket-start can precede the event it contains); point detectors \([0,+300]\,\mathrm{s}\).
